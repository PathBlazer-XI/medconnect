Key facts


According to PROSTATE CANCER UK (accross the UK)
  • Prostate cancer is the most common cancer in men.
  • More than 52,000 men are diagnosed with prostate cancer every year on average – that's 144 men every day.
  • Every 45 minutes one man dies from prostate cancer – that's more than 12,000 men every year.
  • 1 in 8 men will be diagnosed with prostate cancer in their lifetime.
  • Around 490,000 men are living with and after prostate cancer.

What is Prostate?

The prostate, a little walnut-shaped gland found in men and those who were assigned male at birth (AMAB), which grows larger as a man gets older and it is where prostate cancer begins to grow. It is situated in front of the rectum beneath the bladder and surrounds the urethra (the tube that carries urine out of the body). This little gland secretes a fluid that combines with semen to maintain the health of sperm during fertilisation and pregnancy.

One of the most common problem of the prostate is prostate cancer, which is when abnormal cells form and grow in the prostate gland. Not all abnormal growths (tumors), are cancerous (malignant). Some tumors are not cancerous (benign). Benign growths tend to be non life threatening. The majority of prostate cancer patients receive a diagnosis prior to the cancerous cells leaving the prostate gland. Often, at this point, treatment eradicates the malignancy.

What are the types of prostate cancer?

Prostate cancer is most likely an adenocarcinoma ⓘAdenocarcinoma is a type of cancer. It develops in the glands that line your organs. Common forms of adenocarcinoma include breast, stomach, prostate, lung, pancreatic and colorectal cancers. if you are diagnosed with it. Adenocarcinomas originate from cells found in glands that release fluid, such as your prostate. Prostate cancer rarely develops from different kinds of cells.

The following are less frequent forms of prostate cancer:

  • Carcinomas with transitional cells ⓘThis type of cancer usually starts in the bladder and spreads into the prostate. But rarely it can start in the prostate and may spread into the bladder entrance and nearby tissues. This is sometimes called urothelial carcinoma of the prostate.
  • Tumours endocrine in nature
  • SarcomasⓘPrimary prostate sarcoma is a rare malignancy of the prostate with poor prognosis. It originates from the mesoderm in the reproductive tract, and its risk factors may be related to prostatitis, perineal trauma, previous prostate biopsy, and radiation induced.

    • Symptoms of prostate cancer

    Early-stage prostate cancer rarely causes symptoms. These issues may occur as the disease progresses:

    • Frequent, sometimes urgent, need to pee, especially at night.
    • Weak urine flow or flow that starts and stops.
    • Pain or burning when you pee (dysuria).
    • Loss of bladder control (urinary incontinence).
    • Loss of bowel control (fecal incontinence).
    • Painful ejaculation and erectile dysfunction (ED).
    • Blood in semen (hematospermia) or pee.
    • Pain in your low back, hip or chest

    Risk factors for prostate cancer

    Among the most prevalent risk factors are: Age. Growing older raises your risk. If you’re over 50, you have a higher chance of being diagnosed. Prostate cancer affects adults over 65 in about 60% of cases. Ethnicity and race. If you’re African American or Black, your chances are higher. Prostate tumours that are more prone to spread are more likely to occur in you. Prostate cancer is also more likely to develop in people under 50. Prostate cancer in the family history. If a close relative already has prostate cancer, your chances of developing it are two to three times higher. Genetics. If you have Lynch syndrome or inherited the mutant (modified) BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes, which are linked to an elevated risk of breast cancer, you are more vulnerable.

    A few research has show some other risk factors like:

    • Smoking
    • Prostatitis
    • Having a BMI > 30 (having obesity).
    • Sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
    • Exposure to Agent Orange (a chemical used during the Vietnam War).

    Diagnosis and screening tests

    Testing asymptomatic, healthy men for prostate cancer is a contentious issue, with differing opinions among medical organizations regarding whether the benefits of screening outweigh the potential risks. Most medical bodies recommend that men in their 50s engage in discussions with their healthcare providers about the pros and cons of prostate cancer screening. These discussions should involve an assessment of individual risk factors and preferences regarding screening. Should screenings reveal that you may have prostate cancer, you might require more testing or procedures.

    Screening tests for prostate cancer

    Screening tests can determine whether you have prostate cancer symptoms that need further investigation.

    Digital rectal exam
    • Prostate-specific antigen (PSA) blood test: PSA is a protein that is produced by the prostate gland. Elevated PSA readings could suggest malignancy. Additionally, benign illnesses like prostatitis or BPH cause your levels to rise.

    If the tumour is present and fast growing you might need other tests like;

    • Imaging: Your prostate gland can be imaged with an MRI or transrectal ultrasound, revealing any suspicious or potentially cancerous areas. Your provider may use the results of your imaging tests to determine whether to do a biopsy.
    • Biopsy: A medical professional takes a tissue sample using a needle biopsy in order to test it for malignancy in a lab. The only method to accurately detect prostate cancer and determine its level of aggression is through a biopsy. The biopsied tissue may be subjected to genetic testing by your physician. Certain traits (such as mutations) in cancer cells increase their propensity to react to particular therapies.

    Management and treatment

    Your overall health, whether the disease has spread, and how quickly it is spreading are some of the variables that will affect your course of therapy. You may collaborate with other medical professionals, such as urologists, radiation oncologists, and medical oncologists, depending on your treatment plan. Treatment is usually effective in curing most cases of early-stage prostate cancer diagnosis.

    Active surveillance

    To track the progression of cancer, you undergo screenings, scans, and biopsies every one to three years. If the cancer is limited to your prostate, grows slowly, and is not causing symptoms, active surveillance is most effective. Your physician may begin treatment if your problem gets worse.

    Surgery to remove the prostate

    A damaged prostate gland is removed during a radical prostatectomy. When a prostate cancer is effectively removed, it usually does not spread. If your surgeon thinks you would benefit from this procedure, they can advise you on the optimal removal technique.
    With an open radical prostatectomy (open incision), your prostate gland is removed by a single incision made from your belly button to your pubic bone in your abdomen. Compared to less invasive procedures like robotic prostatectomy (Da Vinci surgical incision), this technique is less common.

    Radiation therapy

    Radiation therapy uses high-powered energy to kill cancer cells. Prostate cancer radiation therapy treatments may involve

    • External beam radiation (from oitside the body): External beam radiation therapy uses high-energy beams to target and destroy cancer cells. It's often used after surgery to kill any remaining cancer cells and reduce the risk of recurrence.
    • Brachytherapy (radiation placed into the body): Brachytherapy is a type of internal radiation therapy in which radioactive seeds are inserted into your prostate. With this method, cancer cells are eliminated without harming the surrounding healthy tissue.

    Systemic treatment

    If the cancer has progressed outside of your prostate gland, your physician might suggest systemic treatments. With systemic therapy, chemicals are delivered throughout your body to either kill or stop the growth of cancer cells.

    Hormone therapy

    Testosterone increases the proliferation of cancer cells. Medication is used in hormone therapy to counteract testosterone’s effect in promoting the growth of cancer cells. The medications function by either lowering your testosterone levels or blocking testosterone from reaching cancer cells.

    As an alternative, your doctor can advise an orchiectomy, which involves removing your testicles to stop them from producing testosterone. Those who would prefer not to take medication can choose to get this operation.

    Chemotherapy

    Chemotherapy uses drugs to kill rapidly growing cells, including cancer cells. Chemotherapy can be administered through a vein in your arm, in pill form or both.

    Chemotherapy may be a treatment option for treating prostate cancer that has spread to other areas of the body. Chemotherapy may also be an option for cancers that don't respond to hormone therapy.

    Immunotherapy

    Immunotherapy uses your immune system to fight cancer. Your body's disease-fighting immune system may not attack your cancer because the cancer cells produce proteins that help them hide from the immune system cells. Immunotherapy works by interfering with that process. By fortifying your immune system, immunotherapy improves your body’s capacity to recognise and combat cancerous cells.

    Chemotherapy may be a treatment option for treating prostate cancer that has spread to other areas of the body. Chemotherapy may also be an option for cancers that don't respond to hormone therapy.

    Side effects of prostate cancer treatment

    Each prostate cancer treatment carries potential side effects. Just as prostate cancer manifests differently in each individual, the side effects encountered will also differ. Fortunately, medical professionals strive to minimize these side effects during treatment. Among the most common side effects of prostate cancer treatment are:

  • Urinary incontinence ⓘUrinary incontinence (UI), also known as involuntary urination, is any uncontrolled leakage of urine.
  • Erectile dysfunction ⓘErectile dysfunction, also known as impotence, is defined by difficulty getting and keeping an erection.
  • Infertility ⓘMale infertility is any health issue in a man that lowers the chances of his female partner getting pregnant.

    • These changes have the potential to affect self-esteem and interpersonal relationships. Before making a treatment decision, it's crucial to consult with your doctor to understand the potential side effects and discuss strategies for managing them collaboratively.

    Prevention of prostate cancer

    There's no proven prostate cancer prevention strategy. But you may reduce your risk of prostate cancer by making healthy choices, such as:

    • Geting regular prostate screening
    • Maintaining a healthy weight
    • Exercisimg regularly
    • Eating healthy
    • Quit smoking
