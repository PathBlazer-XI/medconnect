Key facts


According to Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)
  • In 2020, as many as 5.8 million Americans were living with Alzheimer’s disease.
  • The number of people living with the disease doubles every 5 years beyond age 65.
  • This number is projected to nearly triple to 14 million people by 2060.
  • In 2020, alzheimer's care in cost the US economy 1.59 billion US dollars, approximately 50% of these costs are attributable to care provided by informal carers (e.g. family members and close friends), who provide on average 5 hours of care and supervision per day.

What is Alzheimer's disease?

Definition: Alzheimer’s disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by cognitive decline, memory loss, and changes in behavior and personality. It is the most common cause of dementia, accounting for approximately 60-70% of cases.
Pathophysiology: Alzheimer's disease involves the accumulation of abnormal protein deposits in the brain, including beta-amyloid plaques and tau tangles. ⓘTau, the microtubule‐associated protein, forms insoluble filaments that accumulate as neurofibrillary tangles in Alzheimer's disease (AD) and related tauopathies. Under physiological conditions, tau regulates the assembly and maintenance of the structural stability of microtubules. These deposits disrupt communication between neurons, leading to neuronal death and brain atrophy, particularly in areas associated with memory and cognition.
Historical Context: Alzheimer's disease was first described by Alois Alzheimer in 1906, based on the case study of Auguste Deter. Since then, significant advancements have been made in understanding its etiology, pathology, and treatment options..

Statistics from the World Health Organization (WHO) indicate that Alzheimer's Disease is the most common cause of dementia globally, accounting for approximately 60-70% of cases. As of 2021, an estimated 50 million people worldwide are living with dementia, with Alzheimer's Disease being the leading cause in many cases. The prevalence of Alzheimer's Disease is expected to increase significantly in the coming decades due to population aging and the absence of effective disease-modifying treatments.

Symptoms of Alzheimer's disease

Alzheimer disease symptoms include difficulty with many areas of mental function, including:

Cognitive Symptoms

  • Memory loss, especially recent memories.
  • Difficulty in problem-solving and decision-making.
  • Impaired judgment and reasoning.
  • Confusion about time and place.

Behavioral and Psychological Symptoms:

  • Agitation and irritability.
  • Anxiety and depression.
  • Hallucinations and delusions.
  • Changes in personality and social withdrawal.

Physical Symptoms

  • Motor impairments, such as difficulty walking or balancing..
  • Loss of appetite and weight loss.
  • Sleep disturbances.
  • Incontinence.

Who are at risk?

Age: Advanced age is the most significant risk factor for Alzheimer's Disease. The prevalence of Alzheimer's increases with age, doubling approximately every five years after the age of 65. While Alzheimer's can occur in younger individuals, the risk significantly rises with each decade of life.

Obesity: Obesity, characterized by excessive body weight and adipose tissue accumulation, has been identified as a potential risk factor for Alzheimer's Disease. Several studies have demonstrated associations between obesity in midlife and an increased risk of cognitive decline and dementia in later life. Obesity contributes to systemic inflammation, insulin resistance, and vascular dysfunction, all of which are implicated in the pathogenesis of Alzheimer's Disease.

High Blood Pressure (Hypertension): Hypertension, or high blood pressure, is a well-established risk factor for cardiovascular disease and stroke. Emerging evidence suggests that hypertension may also increase the risk of Alzheimer's Disease and cognitive decline. Chronic hypertension contributes to cerebrovascular pathology, including cerebral small vessel disease, white matter lesions, and microvascular dysfunction, which can impair cerebral perfusion and neuronal function. Additionally, hypertension is associated with increased deposition of amyloid-beta protein and tau pathology in the brain, further exacerbating neurodegeneration and cognitive decline.

Diabetes Mellitus: Diabetes mellitus, particularly type 2 diabetes, is associated with an increased risk of Alzheimer's Disease and vascular dementia. Diabetes contributes to systemic inflammation, oxidative stress, and microvascular dysfunction, which can damage the blood-brain barrier and impair cerebral perfusion. Additionally, insulin resistance and dysregulation of glucose metabolism in the brain may promote the accumulation of amyloid-beta protein and tau pathology, contributing to Alzheimer's Disease pathogenesis.

Cardiovascular Disease: Several cardiovascular risk factors, including obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia, and diabetes, are associated with an increased risk of Alzheimer's Disease. Cardiovascular disease contributes to systemic inflammation, endothelial dysfunction, and atherosclerosis, which can impair cerebral perfusion and increase the risk of cerebral small vessel disease and stroke. Additionally, cardiovascular risk factors may exacerbate neurodegenerative processes, including the accumulation of amyloid-beta protein and tau pathology, leading to cognitive decline and dementia.

Lifestyle Factors: Modifiable lifestyle factors, such as physical inactivity, poor diet, smoking, and excessive alcohol consumption, are associated with an increased risk of Alzheimer's Disease. Adopting a healthy lifestyle, including regular exercise, a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and omega-3 fatty acids, smoking cessation, and moderation of alcohol consumption, may help reduce the risk of cognitive decline and dementia.

Diagnosis

Clinical Assessment: Diagnosis of Alzheimer's disease involves a comprehensive evaluation of cognitive function, including memory, language, executive function, and visuospatial abilities. This assessment may include interviews with the patient and caregivers, as well as standardized cognitive tests.

Biomarkers: Biomarkers such as beta-amyloid and tau protein levels in cerebrospinal fluid, as well as neuroimaging techniques like PET scans and MRI, can aid in the diagnosis and monitoring of Alzheimer's disease.

Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5): The DSM-5 criteria outline the clinical symptoms and criteria for diagnosing Alzheimer's Disease, including progressive cognitive decline and impairment in memory and at least one other cognitive domain, such as language, executive function, or visuospatial skills.

Neuropsychological Testing: Neuropsychological assessments evaluate various cognitive domains, including memory, attention, language, and executive function. These tests help in detecting patterns of cognitive impairment characteristic of Alzheimer's Disease and ruling out other potential causes of dementia.

Types/stages of Alzheimer's disease

Alzheimer's disease is currently categorized into various subtypes based on factors such as the severity of symptoms, the inflammatory response observed, and the type of onset or triggering event.

Classification Based on the Severity

Based on the intensity of the typical Alzheimer's symptoms, it can be classified into the following subtypes:

  • Mild Alzheimer's: This phase marks the onset of cognitive decline, resulting in challenges with remembering daily routines such as work tasks and bill payments. While these symptoms are relatively mild, individuals in this stage often struggle to maintain functionality. Tasks that were once completed swiftly now take longer, establishing a noticeable pattern of slowed performance.
  • Moderate Alzheimer's: Due to considerable neuronal damage, individuals with moderate Alzheimer's experience heightened symptoms. Confusion intensifies, and the extent of memory loss renders them increasingly reliant on assistance. Despite retaining physical agility, they struggle with routine tasks as delusions impede sensory processing..
  • Severe Alzheimer's: As the plaques and tangles proliferate, brain cells begin to perish, leading to a reduction in brain tissue volume. Individuals afflicted by this condition often become bedridden and struggle greatly with communication.

These subtypes are more like stages of the disease, and it often progresses from a milder to a more severe form. The sooner the patient is diagnosed with the condition, the better are the chances of treating and preventing its progression.

Management and Treatment

Medical management can improve quality of life for individuals living with Alzheimer’s disease and for their caregivers. There is currently no known cure for Alzheimer’s disease. Treatment aproaches include:

Pharmacological Treatments: Medications such as cholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., donepezil, rivastigmine) and NMDA receptor antagonists (e.g., memantine) can help manage cognitive symptoms and improve quality of life for some individuals with Alzheimer's disease.

Non-Pharmacological Interventions: Non-drug approaches including cognitive stimulation programs, physical exercise, music therapy, and art therapy can provide benefits in terms of cognitive function, mood, and overall well-being.

Caregiver Support: Caregivers play a crucial role in supporting individuals with Alzheimer's disease, and they may require assistance and resources to cope with the demands of caregiving. Support groups, respite care, and caregiver education programs can help caregivers manage stress and provide optimal care for their loved ones.

Prevention of Alzheimer's disease

Lifestyle Modifications: Lifestyle Modifications: Adopting a healthy lifestyle that includes regular physical exercise, a balanced diet rich in fruits and vegetables, adequate sleep, stress management, and social engagement may help reduce the risk of developing Alzheimer's disease.

Cognitive Stimulation: Engaging in mentally stimulating activities such as reading, puzzles, learning new skills, and social interactions can help maintain cognitive function and build cognitive reserve.

Physical Exercise: Regular physical activity, including aerobic exercise and strength training, has been associated with a lower risk of cognitive decline and Alzheimer's disease.

Dietary Considerations: Following a heart-healthy diet such as the Mediterranean diet, which is rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, fish, and healthy fats, may help reduce the risk of Alzheimer's disease.

    Getting a breast MRI involves lying face down on a padded scanning table. The breasts fit into a hollow space in the table. The hollow has coils that get signals from the MRI. The table slides into the large opening of the MRI machine.

    A lumpectomy is surgery to remove a lump in the breast, and some breast tissue in the area around it. The amount of tissue removed depends on the size of the lump.

    Mastectomy
    A “mastectomy” is surgery to remove the whole breast, usually including the nipple. The goal of a mastectomy is to remove the cancer in the breast and to try prevent the spread of cancer. During a total mastectomy, the surgeon removes the breast tissue, nipple, areola and skin. This procedure also is known as a simple mastectomy. Other mastectomy procedures may leave some parts of the breast, such as the skin or the nipple. Surgery to create a new breast is optional. It can be done at the same time as mastectomy surgery or it can be done later.

    Sentinel lymph node biopsy
    This procedure involves removing a few lymph nodes to determine whether the cancer has spread beyond the breast. The surgeon uses a harmless dye and a weak radioactive solution to locate the sentinel nodes. The nodes are removed and tested for signs of cancer.
