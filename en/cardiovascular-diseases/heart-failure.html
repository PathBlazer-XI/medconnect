Heart failure

Heart failure directly accounts for about 8.5% of all heart disease deaths in the United States.
Last updated

Key facts (USA)


According to Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)
  • About 6.2 million adults in the United States have heart failure.
  • In 2018, heart failure was mentioned on 379,800 death certificates (13.4%).
  • Heart failure costs the nation an estimated $30.7 billion in 2012.2 This total includes the cost of health care services, medicines to treat heart failure, and missed days of work.

What is Heart failure?

Heart failure, sometimes referred to as congestive heart failure, is a medical illness that arises when the heart is unable to pump enough blood to meet the demands of the body. This may occur if your heart is unable to pump enough blood. It may also occur if your heart is not strong enough to pump blood adequately.

Heart failure does not mean your heart has stopped working. But instead, It is unable to provide sufficient blood flow to meet metabolic requirements or accommodate systemic venous return

Signs & Symptoms of Heart failure

The signs and symptoms of heart failure (HF) occur due to the clinical consequences of insufficient cardiac output and ineffective venous return. These signs and symptoms include:

  • Extreme tiredness or weakness
  • Chest pain (Especially during exertion).
  • Shortness of breath.
  • Rapid changes in weight.
  • Feeling light-headed /Dizziness.
  • Swelling in the ankles, feet, legs or tummy.
  • Rapid or irregular heartbeat.
  • Trouble breathing when lying down.

What are the risk factors?

Several medical conditions can heighten the likelihood of developing heart failure. These conditions encompass a range of cardiovascular and systemic health issues which includes:

  • Coronary artery disease (CAD) (the most common type of heart disease) and heart attacks
  • Diabetes
  • Obesity
  • High blood pressure

Engaging in unhealthy habits can significantly elevate the likelihood of developing heart failure, particularly among individuals already afflicted with any of the aforementioned conditions. These detrimental behaviors encompass a wide range of factors that can exacerbate heart health risks, such as:

  • Poor dietary choices high in saturated fats, sodium, and processed sugars.
  • Smoking tobacco or exposure to secondhand smoke.
  • Excessive alcohol consumption.
  • Not getting enough physical activity.

Diagnosis

Ejection fraction: measures how much blood the heart is pumping with each beat. This helps diagnose the type of heart failure you have and guides your treatment. If 40% or less of the blood in your left ventricle is pumped out in one beat, you have heart failure with reduced ejection fraction.

Resting or exercise electrocardiogram (also known as EKG, ECG, or stress test): These provide valuable information about the heart's electrical activity, rhythm, and response to stress or exertion, aiding clinicians in evaluating cardiac function and identifying underlying conditions contributing to heart failure. However, it's important to note that while ECG tests are helpful diagnostic tools, they are often used in conjunction with other tests and clinical assessments to make a comprehensive diagnosis of heart failure.

Echocardiogram: This is diagnostic test commonly used to evaluate heart function and structure, including its chambers, valves, and blood flow patterns. It uses high-frequency sound waves (ultrasound) to create detailed images of the heart in real-time. It is a non-invasive, safe, and effective tool for diagnosing heart failure and assessing its severity, guiding treatment decisions, and monitoring the response to treatment over time.

Computed tomography (CT) scan: Computed tomography (CT) scan is a diagnostic imaging technique that utilizes X-rays and computer processing to create detailed cross-sectional images of the body. In the context of heart failure diagnosis, CT scans can provide valuable information about the structure and function of the heart, as well as any associated abnormalities.

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scan: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a diagnostic imaging technique that uses a strong magnetic field and radio waves to generate detailed images of the body's internal structures. In the context of heart failure diagnosis, cardiac MRI specifically focuses on capturing images of the heart to assess its structure, function, and blood flow.

Positron Emission Tomography (PET) scan: This is a diagnostic imaging technique used to evaluate various functions within the body, including heart function. In the context of heart failure, PET scans can provide valuable information about the heart's metabolism, blood flow, and viability of heart tissue.

Biopsy or catheterization of the heart and arteries: Thesr are invasive diagnostic procedures used to assess heart failure and its underlying causes. Both of these procedures play crucial roles in diagnosing heart failure and guiding treatment decisions, although they carry some risks and are typically reserved for cases where non-invasive tests are inconclusive or further information is needed for accurate diagnosis and management.

Types of Heart Failure

There are several types of heart failure, which can be categorized based on the part of the heart that is primarily affected or the underlying cause. Here are the main types.

Left-sided heart failure

  • Systolic heart failure: Also known as reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), this occurs when the left ventricle of the heart is weakened and unable to contract effectively, leading to decreased pumping ability and reduced ejection fraction (the percentage of blood pumped out with each heartbeat).
  • Diastolic heart failure: Also called preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF), this type occurs when the left ventricle becomes stiff and less compliant, impairing its ability to relax and fill properly during the relaxation phase (diastole) of the cardiac cycle.

Right-sided heart failure

Right-sided heart failure often occurs as a consequence of left-sided heart failure. It happens when the right ventricle is unable to effectively pump blood to the lungs for oxygenation, leading to fluid accumulation in the body, particularly in the legs, ankles, and abdomen.

Acute heart failure

This type of heart failure develops suddenly and requires urgent medical attention. It can occur as a result of a heart attack, severe arrhythmias, or acute valve dysfunction, among other causes.

Chronic heart failure

Chronic heart failure develops gradually over time and is often characterized by symptoms such as fatigue, shortness of breath, and swelling. It can result from various underlying conditions, including coronary artery disease, hypertension, cardiomyopathy, and valvular heart disease.

Understanding the type of heart failure a patient has is crucial for guiding treatment decisions and optimizing outcomes. Treatment approaches may include medications, lifestyle modifications, device therapy (such as implantable cardioverter-defibrillators or cardiac resynchronization therapy), and, in some cases, surgical interventions like heart valve repair or replacement or coronary artery bypass grafting.

Stages of Heart failure

STAGE A: At risk for heart failure

People who are at risk for heart failure but do not yet have symptoms or structural or functional heart disease. Risk factors for people in this stage include hypertension, coronary vascular disease, diabetes, obesity, exposure to cardio toxic agents, genetic variants for cardiomyopathy and family history of cardiomyopathy.

STAGE B: Pre-heart failure

People without current or previous symptoms of heart failure but with either structural heart disease, increased filling pressures in the heart or other risk factors.

Stage C: Symptomatic Heart Failure

People with current or previous symptoms of heart failure. Which may include shortness of breath, patigue and weakness, swelling (Edema) particularly in the legs, ankles, feet, or abdomen. Persistent coughing or Wheezing.

Stage D: Advanced Heart Failure

People in in this stage experience symptoms that significantly interfere with daily life functions or lead to repeated hospitalizations despite receiving appropriate medical treatment. This stage may also include individuals who are candidates for advanced heart failure therapies, such as heart transplantation, mechanical circulatory support (e.g., ventricular assist devices), or palliative care.

Understanding these stages helps healthcare providers determine appropriate interventions, monitor disease progression, and optimize patient outcomes through timely management and treatment strategies tailored to each stage of heart failure.

Management and treatment

Treatment of heart failure may depend on the cause. Treatment often includes lifestyle changes and medicines.

Healthy lifestyle changes

  • Lower your sodium (salt) intake.
  • Aim for a healthy weight since extra weight can make your heart work harder.
  • Get regular physical activity.
  • Quit smoking.
  • Avoid or limit alcohol.
  • Manage contributing risk factors.
  • Manage stress.
  • Get good-quality sleep.

Medications

A combination of medicines may be used to treat heart failure. The specific medicines used depend on the cause of heart failure and the symptoms. Medicines to treat heart failure include:

Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors: These drugs relax blood vessels to lower blood pressure, improve blood flow and decrease the strain on the heart. Examples include enalapril (Vasotec, Epaned), lisinopril (Zestril, Qbrelis) and captopril.

Angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs): These drugs have many of the same benefits as ACE inhibitors. They may be an option for people who can't tolerate ACE inhibitors. They include losartan (Cozaar), valsartan (Diovan) and candesartan (Atacand).

Angiotensin receptor plus neprilysin inhibitors (ARNIs): This medicine uses two blood pressure drugs to treat heart failure. The combination medicine is sacubitril-valsartan (Entresto). It's used to treat some people with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction. It may help prevent the need for a hospital stay in those people. Beta blockers: These medicines slow the heart rate and lower blood pressure. They reduce the symptoms of heart failure and help the heart work better. If you have heart failure, beta blockers may help you live longer. Examples include carvedilol (Coreg), metoprolol (Lopressor, Toprol-XL, Kapspargo Sprinkle) and bisoprolol.

Diuretics: Often called water pills, these medicines make you urinate more frequently. This helps prevent fluid buildup in your body. Diuretics, such as furosemide (Lasix, Furoscix), also decrease fluid in the lungs, so it's easier to breathe. Some diuretics make the body lose potassium and magnesium. Your health care provider may recommend supplements to treat this. If you're taking a diuretic, you may have regular blood tests to check your potassium and magnesium levels.

Surgery or otherprocedures

Surgery or other treatment to place a heart device may be recommended to treat the problem that led to heart failure. Surgery or other procedures for heart failure may include:

Coronary bypass surgery: You may need this surgery if severely blocked arteries are causing your heart failure. The surgery involves taking a healthy blood vessel from the leg, arm or chest and connecting it below and above the blocked arteries in the heart. The new pathway improves blood flow to the heart muscle.

Heart valve repair or replacement.: This is done If a damaged heart valve causes heart failure. Heart valve repair or replacement may be done as open-heart or minimally invasive surgery.

Implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD): An ICD is used to prevent complications of heart failure. It isn't a treatment for heart failure itself. An ICD is a device similar to a pacemaker. It's implanted under the skin in the chest with wires leading through the veins and into the heart. he ICD monitors the heartbeat. If the heart starts beating at a dangerous rhythm, the ICD tries to correct the beat. If the heart stops, the device shocks it back into regular rhythm. An ICD can also work as a pacemaker and speed up a slow heartbeat.

Heart transplant: Some people have such severe heart failure that surgery or medicines don't help. These people may need to have their hearts replaced with a healthy donor heart. A heart transplant isn't the right treatment for everyone. A team of health care providers at a transplant center helps determine whether the procedure may be safe and beneficial for you.

    Mastectomy
    A “mastectomy” is surgery to remove the whole breast, usually including the nipple. The goal of a mastectomy is to remove the cancer in the breast and to try prevent the spread of cancer. During a total mastectomy, the surgeon removes the breast tissue, nipple, areola and skin. This procedure also is known as a simple mastectomy. Other mastectomy procedures may leave some parts of the breast, such as the skin or the nipple. Surgery to create a new breast is optional. It can be done at the same time as mastectomy surgery or it can be done later.

    Sentinel lymph node biopsy
    This procedure involves removing a few lymph nodes to determine whether the cancer has spread beyond the breast. The surgeon uses a harmless dye and a weak radioactive solution to locate the sentinel nodes. The nodes are removed and tested for signs of cancer.
